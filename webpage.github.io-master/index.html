About Me

        Hey! My name is Oscar Molina and I'm a fourth-year Biochemistry student at UCLA. As a first generation college student, I was not exposed as to what someone in my field did after college. During my Sophomore year, I was fortunate to join Dr. Albert J. Courey's Lab. With two years of balancing 15+ hours of lab with participating in two summers of undergraduate summer research, I am excited to move onto the next step in my career and pursue a PhD in Biochemistry, Chemical Biology, or Molecular Biology. My experience as an underrepresented researcher at UCLA and Harvard has motivated me to become an academic research professor to improve and create more pipelines for underrepresented students in research to pursue graduate studies in science.

Undergraduate Research

Courey Lab

Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry, UCLA

January 2018 - Current

Undergraduate Student Researcher

We study the molecular mechanisms during development in our model organism of Drosophila melanogaster. My role in the lab has been to elucidate the spatiotemporal mechanism of SUMO within the Ras/MAPK pathway which has been previously shown to affect signaling in early Drosophila embryo development. I have designed and implemented purification strategies of covalently SUMO conjugated proteins and developed proteomic workflows to identify both SUMO conjugated proteins and specific covalently modified residues in order to have clear targets for site-directed mutagenesis of proteins involved in Ras signaling. Also, I have incorporated genetic and biochemical techniques to disrupt non-covalent interactions between SUMO and Ras which we have shown to interact non-covalently. I am currently establishing the regulating mechanism of the non-covalent interaction between SUMO and Ras on Ras signaling.

10-week Summer Research

Woo Lab

Department of Chemistry and Chemical Biology, Harvard University

June 2019 - August 2019

10-week Summer Researcher

We study the post-translation modification (PTM) and nutrient sensor, O-linked N-acetylglucosamine (O-GlcNAc), which modifies >3,000 proteins and is a key PTM that coordinates cellular responses determining cellular fate. O-GlcNAc Transferase (OGT) is the essential enzyme solely responsible for the addition of O-GlcNAc on proteins, but how OGT select its proteins to modify under certain stimuli is unknown. My role was to establish how OGT selects proteins to modify under heat stress. Over the summer, I applied chemo-enzymatic labeling and isotope targeted labeling proteomics to describe the O-GlcNAc proteome under heat stress in U2OS cells. I also implemented nanobody technology for proximity induced interaction between SUMO-conjugating enzyme-UBC9 and O-GlcNAc Transferase (OGT) for selective SUMO conjugation of OGT in U2OS and HEK 293T cells. Using immunofluorescence and fluorescence microscopy I was able to show OGT localized to the cytoplasm with direct covalent SUMOylation and in contrast global increase of SUMOylated proteins under heat stress caused OGT to localize back into the nucleus.
